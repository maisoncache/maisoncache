              Nettle
              Urtica dioica
              
            


            
            

              from the keeper

              🪴
              Grow It
              ▾
            

            

              
              

                Southern Hemisphere — Autumn
                
Nettle wants a moist, rich corner you've slightly given up on. Near the compost heap is ideal. Plant now through early May — the cooler months suit it, though it will die back in frost and return, insistently, in spring.

                
It spreads by rhizome. Give it a boundary you mean. An old metal bucket buried to the rim works well. It will test the boundary. Water deeply once a week; less once established. It doesn't want fuss. It wants to be left alone to do what it does.

                
Harvest with gloves, always. Snip young tops — the first six leaves. Older leaves get coarse and bitter. Early morning, before the sun has had them.

              

              

                Northern Hemisphere — Autumn
                
Nettle thrives planted now, before the first hard frost. Prepare a moist, nitrogen-rich spot — alongside a compost heap, or the shadier edge of a kitchen garden. It wants to overwinter quietly and surge back in March.

                
Contain it — it spreads underground with real ambition. A buried barrier at least 20cm deep will hold it, mostly. Plant it once. It will stay. Water in well and leave it. It doesn't need watching.

                
Harvest gloved in spring, taking only the young tops. First six leaves, cut before flowering. Blanching instantly removes the sting — boiling water or a dry pan, thirty seconds, done.

              

              
            

          


          

            

              🍵
              Use It
              ▾
            

            

              
              In the kitchen
              
Treat nettle exactly like spinach, once blanched. Nettle soup — potato, onion, stock, a handful of young tops — is genuinely one of the quietest, most nourishing things you can make. Nettle pesto: blanched nettle, walnuts, hard cheese, olive oil, lemon. Darker and more mineral than basil. Better, some would say.

              
Nettle tea: pour boiling water over fresh or dried leaves. Leave five minutes. Drink it plain or with honey. It tastes like green, like iron, like something that means business.

              On the body
              
A nettle hair rinse strengthens the scalp and adds shine — steep a handful of dried nettle in boiling water for twenty minutes, cool, strain, pour through hair after washing. Leave it in. Your grandmother knew this.

              
As a skin tonic: cooled nettle tea patted onto oily skin tightens pores without stripping. It's astringent, mineral-rich, and underestimated.

              In scent
              
Nettle reads as green, sharp, and mineral in fragrance — the smell of something living that doesn't apologise for it. It appears in fougères and fresh green compositions as a structural note, giving the kind of outdoors that is specifically early morning after rain. If you want to capture it: crush a young leaf between your fingers and breathe in slowly.

              
            

          


          

            

              📖
              Know It
              ▾
            

            

              
              
Nettle is one of the oldest cultivated plants in the world, used before recorded cultivation began. The Romans brought it to Britain and reportedly beat their limbs with it to keep warm in the northern cold — urtication, it's called, and it's still used in some folk medicine traditions for arthritis.

              
In folk magic and witchcraft, nettle is a protection plant. Hung at the door, it was believed to keep ill intent outside. Worn or carried, it was said to deflect curses back to their sender. The logic being: something that stings so reliably can hold a boundary. It knows what it is. It doesn't apologise for it.

              
Norse mythology gives nettle to Thor — the plant of lightning, storm, and masculine vitality. Germanic traditions used nettle thread to weave fairy-proof linen. Hans Christian Andersen's The Wild Swans — the sister who weaves nettle shirts to break her brothers' curse — is folklore as instruction: painful, careful work, done in silence, for love.

              
The sting itself is a mixture of formic acid, histamine, and serotonin. The body's response — heat, redness, a tingling that lingers — is, in miniature, very like the sensation of adrenaline. It wakes you up. It reminds you that you're in a body that responds.

              
            

          


          

            

              🔗
              Pair It
              ▾
            

            

              
              In the Hearth
              
The nettle soup mentioned above pairs directly with the Hearth's winter bundle. Make it on a waning moon — the week the year starts moving toward rest. Eat it alone or with someone who also doesn't need to explain why they're tired. It fills something that toast doesn't.

              In the Atelier
              
If you want to understand how nettle reads in perfume, try any classic fougère — Brut, Fahrenheit, Drakkar Noir — the green-lavender-oak moss family that nettle supports. Or go directly to something contemporary like Diptyque's Virgilio — fig and green nettle, slightly bitter, spring garden after rain. Smell it and then crush a nettle leaf. You'll hear the connection.

              A note for the season
              
Nettle grows in disturbed ground. Wherever the soil has been moved, broken, left uncertain — there it comes. The old name for it is "ortie" in French — from the Latin urtica, to burn. But the other meaning of disturbed ground is: this is where something is happening. This is where the change is. Nettle knows it. It shows up there first.
